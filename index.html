Johnson & Johnson University Case Competition

March 2020 - April 2020

In the Spring of 2020, a group of fellow IBE students and I participated in the Johnson & Johnson University Case Competition. In the given fictional scenario, we were a team at Johnson & Johnson tasked with deciding the logistics of the acquisition of a small, research-oriented company. Our decisions involved which of three companies to choose from, when to buy them, which projects to adopt, which marketing strategies to pursue, and more. Each decision had a financial impact that could be seen in a provided interactive Excel worksheet showing the project's cash flows, net present value, and other metrics. Our decisions had to provide a financially sound project, adequate product market share, and consistency with the company credo. After taking time to discuss each decision, our team found a strategy that balanced each aspect of the project nicely. After presenting our plan to the judges, we were given "a close second" place in the competition. This experience offered insight into corporate finance decision-making, helped me develop my teamwork skills through our groupâ€™s involved discussions, and reminded me about the effectiveness of a team with diverse thought processes.

IBE Business Game

August 2020 - December 2020

In the Fall of 2020, I took an IBE course that involved learning how to use Microsoft Excel for optimization and modeling in a varitey of business applications. We were then able to directly apply these concepts in the second part of the course: a business simulation game.

The business game involved our class forming 12 teams, or companies, that would compete for the largest amount of net assets by the end of the semester. Every team started with the identical assets and were in direct competition with each other, selling identical units. Each week, all of the teams decided how to price units across 12 cities, whether we want to build new plants or warehouses, and how to invest our cash, whether itâ€™s buying stocks in other companies, certificates of deposit, cost control programs, adversitzing, and R&D.

My team took a conservative approach thoughout the game by keeping moderate prices, safe stock investments, and inventory. We used some of the optimization strategies we learned in class to aid in certain decisions, such as our weekly pricing strategy. In the trial run of the simulation, our team placed first, and in the real simulation, we placed third. Our moderate strategy was rewarding and proved the effectiveness of a well-thought and analyzed plan.

IBE Freshman Workshop

January 2019 - May 2019

During my freshman year, I took an IBE-specific course that discussed fundamentals of entrepreneurship. Over the last seven weeks of the course, our class was split into groups and tasked with developing a product to solve some kind of problem. For example, my group had to design a product that would effectively remove snow and ice from the cars of daily commuters the morning after a snowstorm. The seven-week-long project involved talking with our target customers, industry professionals, possible manufacturers, Lehigh professors, and others to ultimately design a prototype and business plan to bring the product to market. We then presented this product and plan to professors, peers, and family.

Our original design for the product resembled a small snowblower that the user could move over the top of their car; however, we realized that this design was not realistic for a number of reasons, such as power consumption and usability. After trying to find ways to work around our problems, we realized we needed a completely new design. I mention this design pivot because learning that it is sometimes necessary to change direction entirely for the best overall outcome was one of the biggest lessons that I learned while working on the project.

We named our final product Avalanche. This snow shovel shown in the image below came with a number of features that make it effective. Its shape as shown is ideal for pushing snow off a car. The square face can also be rotated parallel to the shaft and turn the device into a shovel for digging a car out of a snow pile. The handle can be taken out to reveal a pointed end used for breaking up ice. Also, the user can attach a "de-icing" slip to a roller at the base of the square face to help melt stuck-on ice.

This project was very involved, and I enjoyed getting the hands-on experience of working through an entrepreneurial process. It was a great exercise to understand much of what goes into starting a business. Many of the skills I developed and insights I gathered while working on this project I will carry with me throughout my professional career.
